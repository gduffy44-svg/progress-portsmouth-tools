A course for civic housing advocates

Catalyzing Local Housing Reform

A field-tested approach from Progress Portsmouth — written for advocates in other communities trying to move their cities from rhetoric to a serious housing reform agenda. Most of what worked in Portsmouth was contingent. The method is replicable. The outcomes are not.

Before reading: use the Translate to your community diagnostic in the sidebar. The course is grounded in a specific small New England city; the diagnostic flags which modules will need adaptation for yours.

Module 01 of 08~4 min

Orientation: read this first

Quick read

This course teaches you how to read your own community and build a method that fits it. It does not teach you to copy Portsmouth.

Before working through the modules, take five minutes with the Translate to your community diagnostic. Portsmouth is a small New England city — population ~22,000, Council-Manager government, at-large representation. None of those are universal. The diagnostic flags which modules will need adaptation for your community before you read them.

This course distills how Progress Portsmouth — a small civic group with no formal authority — helped Portsmouth, NH adopt a comprehensive Housing Action Plan. It is written for advocates in other communities trying to do similar work.

Most of what worked in Portsmouth was contingent. A specific Zoning Board denial of a micro-unit project created a public opening. Two members of City Council were willing to champion housing reform. City staff were operational partners, not adversaries. The two Progress Portsmouth founders had complementary skills — civic strategy and parcel-level GIS analysis. None of that is replicable on demand.

What is transferable is a posture and a sequence: how to recognize an opening, how to build credibility before influence, how to position outside the formal structure while staying adjacent to it, and how to build civic infrastructure that a city can absorb.

Three paths through this material

  • Skim (~10 min) — civic-minded resident with limited time. Read only the orange "Quick read" panels. Use the path picker in the sidebar to filter.
  • Read (~45 min) — existing advocacy group leveling up. Read everything. Use the green diagnostic exercises if helpful.
  • Work through (~3 hrs) — solo organizer starting from zero. One module per workday, complete the exercises, then revisit.
Before you start

Write down, in two sentences, what you actually want.

"More housing" is not specific enough. "A city-adopted set of zoning amendments that legalize multi-family by right in three districts within 24 months" is. The clearer your target, the easier the rest of this course is to apply. Revisit and revise this sentence at the end of each module.

Module 02 of 08~6 min

Diagnose your community

Quick read

There's a difference between "we should do something" and "this community can do something right now." Tell the truth about which one you're in.

Before you decide what to do, figure out where you are. Many advocates skip this step and end up running campaigns their community can't yet support. The work then fails for reasons that look like opposition but are actually about timing and readiness.

Three diagnostic questions

  1. Is there a recent specific event — a denied project, a contentious zoning vote, a public statement from an elected official, a news story — that has put housing on the local agenda?
  2. Is there at least one elected official who has signaled openness to reform, ideally publicly?
  3. Are city staff (planning, building, assessing) reachable as professionals — or is the relationship adversarial?

If you answered no to two or more, your work is foundation-laying, not reform-pushing. Pretending otherwise produces frustration.

Common patterns

  • Activated and stuck. Events have created urgency but no champion exists. Your work is champion identification.
  • Champion without urgency. A friendly councilor exists but housing isn't on the agenda. Your work is visibility — making the problem legible.
  • Capacity gap. Both exist but staff are overwhelmed. Your value is reducing their workload, not adding to it.
  • Locked. None of the three are present. Your work is base-building, not reform-pushing. This is a multi-year posture.
Portsmouth context

Portsmouth was "activated and stuck" until early 2025: a Zoning Board denial of a micro-unit project had created public attention, but no formal champion had emerged. The pivotal move was Progress Portsmouth approaching individual councilors with parcel-level data and a draft framework, which gave at least one councilor enough material to take the issue up publicly. Without the prior denial, the same outreach would likely have produced polite acknowledgment and nothing else.

Diagnostic

Answer these in writing

  1. Name the most recent housing-relevant public event in your community. If you can't name one in the last 12 months, the agenda isn't yet activated.
  2. Name one elected official who has spoken publicly about housing in the last 12 months. If you can't, you don't yet have a champion candidate.
  3. Name one professional staff member (planning, assessing, code) you could reach by email tomorrow. If you can't, build that relationship before anything else.

Whichever blank you find first is your starting point — not the policy reform you wanted to push.

Module 03 of 08~7 min

Position yourself

Quick read

Pick your lane on purpose. The wrong position will block you regardless of how good your work is.

There are three positions a housing advocate can take. They are not interchangeable, and most advocates default into a position rather than choose one.

Outside resource

Unaffiliated with the city. No formal authority. Works at the direction of elected officials.

Best for
Producing rigorous analysis a city can absorb without political baggage.
Cost
Slow to build credibility. No permanent seat. Every piece of access has to be re-earned.

Adjacent partner

Formal organization (often 501(c)(3) or (c)(4)). Participates in formal processes.

Best for
Sustained public-facing advocacy, fundraising, multi-year campaigns.
Cost
Board, IRS, donor constraints. Can't easily change direction once a strategy is set.

Inside role

Commission member, candidate, council member. Holds formal authority.

Best for
Direct influence on votes and process design.
Cost
Bound by procedure and politics. Loses freedom to reframe issues publicly.

Most advocates default to "adjacent partner" because that's what civic groups usually look like, then wonder why they can't move fast or speak frankly. Most advocates avoid "outside resource" because it sounds insubstantial — but that's exactly why it can be useful. An outside resource is harder to dismiss as a faction; the analysis stands or falls on its own merits.

Trap

Trying to occupy two positions at once. An advocate who is also on the planning board confuses both their colleagues and the public. Pick one position; if you change, change deliberately and announce it.

Choose

Which position fits your situation?

  1. Which of your skills is hardest to replace? (Analysis, organizing, public speaking, relationships, fundraising.) Different positions reward different strengths.
  2. What's your time horizon? Outside resource and inside role both require multi-year commitment. Adjacent partner can work shorter-term.
  3. How much constraint can you tolerate? An adjacent partner with a board has the most. An outside resource has the least.
Module 04 of 08~5 min

Build credibility before influence

Quick read

Earn the right to be heard before you ask to be heard. Six months of credible work first; access second.

The temptation is to skip this step. Don't. Credibility for housing advocates comes from three sources, and access without these three exposes the gaps rather than producing influence.

The three sources

  • Evidence. Data, analysis, parcel-level work, fiscal modeling, citations. Numbers that withstand scrutiny — and that you can defend when challenged in a public meeting.
  • Neutrality of presentation. Framing that an opponent could read without finding strawmen or loaded language. This does not mean you have no position. It means your work product can be trusted by people who disagree with your conclusions.
  • Reliability. Showing up to meetings, finishing what you said you'd finish, declining to overclaim. Boring and underrated.

Your first six months should produce one credible artifact — a rigorous analysis, a clean data tool, a useful map — before you ask for anything. Skip this step and you'll be classified as another loud advocate. Once classified that way, reclassification is expensive.

Honesty trap

"Neutral framing" is not the same as "no position." If you genuinely have no position, you're a researcher, not an advocate. If you have a position, design your work so the analysis is independent of your conclusions — then say what you think. The two products serve different purposes; don't blend them in a single document.

Portsmouth context

Progress Portsmouth's first credibility-building artifact was a parcel-by-parcel yield analysis of the city's residential zones — what the existing zoning permits, what alternative zoning would permit, and where the gap is largest. It took months. It was deliberately published with no policy recommendations attached. The recommendations came separately, later, after the analysis itself had been read and used by people across the political spectrum.

Module 05 of 08~6 min

Map the ecosystem

Quick read

Know everyone who can help or block. Most of them aren't elected.

Most advocates over-focus on elected officials and under-focus on everyone else who actually shapes outcomes. The full ecosystem usually includes:

  • Elected officials. Vary in housing literacy and willingness. Identify champions, fence-sitters, and opponents — and accept that the list will change with each election.
  • Professional staff. Planning, building, assessing, legal. Their workload is often the binding constraint on what's possible. They outlast officials.
  • Local developers. Full range from small infill builders to large mixed-use developers. Their lived experience of your zoning is data you don't have.
  • Nonprofit housing developers. Different incentives than for-profit. Potential project partners for affordable units. Often statewide or regional in scope.
  • State housing finance agencies and coalitions. Funding, technical assistance, statewide policy context. Free expertise that advocates routinely ignore.
  • Regional workforce or housing coalitions. Convening power, network access, sometimes deployment infrastructure for materials you produce.
  • Existing residents and neighborhoods. Including those who oppose reform. Understanding their concerns is required, not optional.

For each, ask: What do they want? What do they fear? What do they know that I don't? If you can't answer those three questions for a stakeholder, you don't yet understand them.

Common mistakes

  • Treating opponents as a monolith. Most opposition is driven by specific concerns — parking, neighborhood character, a specific project, distrust of process — that are addressable individually. The composite "they" doesn't exist.
  • Skipping staff. A planning director who thinks your work is honest will quietly include you for years. A planning director who thinks you're sloppy will quietly exclude you for years. The relationship is asymmetric.
  • Romanticizing developers. Most are running businesses; some align with your goals and some don't. Both can be useful. Neither is your friend in the way an organizer might want them to be.
  • Underusing state agencies. State housing finance agencies routinely offer free presentations, data, and technical assistance that advocates spend months trying to produce themselves.
Exercise

Build a stakeholder map

List every actor in the ecosystem above for your community. For each, write one sentence answering each of the three questions: what they want, what they fear, what they know. If a row is blank, that's a research task — not a guess to fill in.

Update this map quarterly. People change positions; your understanding goes stale faster than you think.

Module 06 of 08~6 min

Build civic infrastructure

Quick read

Make things that outlast you. Op-eds and rallies have a shelf life of days. Tools and data persist.

Most housing advocacy produces ephemeral outputs: op-eds, public testimony, social posts, rallies, comment letters. They have a shelf life of days. They are useful in the moment but they don't compound.

Civic infrastructure is different. It is tools, data sources, and educational materials that persist and can eventually be absorbed by the city. Examples from Progress Portsmouth:

  • A parcel yield analysis showing how many units could be added under different zoning scenarios
  • A learning library of explainers on local zoning, state housing law, and reform options
  • A public Housing Action Plan tracker that lets anyone see what's been adopted and what's pending
  • Periodic housing market snapshots

Why this matters: at some point your group will get tired, disagree internally, or pivot. If your output was 50 op-eds, none of it survives that moment. If your output was a tracking tool the city now maintains, the work outlives the group.

Standards for civic infrastructure

  • Useful to people who disagree with you
  • Free of advocacy framing in the artifact itself (the framing is in your separate advocacy products)
  • Maintainable by people other than you
  • Eventually transferable to the city or another permanent home
Trap

Building tools to feel productive without checking whether anyone uses them. Before you build, find one specific person — a councilor, a staff member, a developer — who will use a draft version, and keep showing them iterations. If no one is asking for it, the artifact is probably for you, not for them.

Portsmouth context

The Housing Action Plan Explorer began as a working document Progress Portsmouth used internally to organize policy items. Iterations happened in public meetings — councilors and staff would see the tool, request a feature, and the request would be in the next version a week later. Tools built this way get used. Tools built without that feedback loop usually don't.

Module 07 of 08~5 min

Work through officials, not parallel to them

Quick read

Once it's adopted, it's theirs. Act accordingly.

Once you have a champion and the issue is on the agenda, the question changes: how do you work with elected officials without becoming their staff or their opponent?

Three principles

Direction, not parallel action.

Once an elected body has taken up an issue, your work should support what they're doing — not run a separate process that competes with theirs. Parallel processes are usually read as criticism even when they're not intended that way. If a Housing Committee is doing triage, do not publish your own triage.

Outside resource means available, not embedded.

You attend meetings as a non-voting resource. You answer questions when asked. You don't draft policy unless asked. The line between "helpful outside expert" and "uncompensated staff" is thin and worth maintaining — embedded advocates lose the independence that made them useful in the first place.

Public credit goes to officials, not you.

Anything you build that is adopted should be credited to the body that adopted it. Your name on a successful reform is a short-term win and a long-term cost — it converts policy into a partisan artifact that can be reversed by the next election.

Trap

Resentment about not getting credit. The work is the credit. If you need public attribution to feel the effort was worth it, your motivation will eventually misalign with the work.

Reflect

Test yourself

If your group's most important achievement this year were credited entirely to the City Council, with your name nowhere on it, would you still be glad it happened? If the honest answer is no, you have a position-and-motivation alignment problem to work on before you have an advocacy problem.

Module 08 of 08~4 min

Plan the handoff

Quick read

Build for a successor you haven't met. The end state of good advocacy is the advocate becoming unnecessary.

Housing advocacy that depends on one person or one small group is fragile. Reform that survives requires institutional ownership — meaning the tools, the data, the relationships, and the maintenance routines exist outside your group.

Practical handoff steps

  • Document everything in formats the city can edit. Avoid proprietary tools or skills only you have. If your tracker is a custom system only one person knows how to update, it's a liability dressed up as an asset.
  • Identify a staff or committee owner before transferring anything. "We'd like to give this to the city" is a polite gesture; "Director X has agreed to take this on" is a transfer.
  • Resolve security and authorization issues before handoff. Your tools probably have shortcuts that work because you trust yourself; the city can't operate that way. Audit credentials, hard-coded passwords, and any access patterns that depend on you specifically.
  • Plan for your departure as if it could happen unexpectedly. It might. Illness, burnout, relocation, disagreement — any of these can end an advocacy group's run with no warning.
Trap

Hero narrative. The temptation to be the indispensable expert is strong and corrosive. It produces fragile reform, dependent successors, and eventually burnout. Resist it from the start, not at the end.

Closing~3 min

What this course doesn't teach

It is worth being explicit about scope, because this course is sometimes mistaken for a complete advocacy curriculum. It is not.

This course doesn't teach:

  • How to organize protests or run pressure campaigns. Those are different methods with different premises and different ethics. They can complement the approach in this course; they're not substitutes for it.
  • How to build a 501(c)(3) or (c)(4). If you're going that route, this course is incomplete — you need governance, finance, and compliance content beyond its scope.
  • How to win electoral races. Different skill set, different timeline, different ethics around partisanship.
  • How to handle hostile city governments. Portsmouth's was receptive. If yours is not, your sequence and tools will need to be different — possibly starting with the "Locked" pattern in Module 02 and building from there over years.

It does teach: how a small civic group with no formal authority can help its community produce a serious, comprehensive housing reform document — and how to do it without burning relationships or building a fragile structure.

The method is replicable. The outcomes are not guaranteed. Anyone who promises you both is selling something.

Final exercise

Revisit your two-sentence target from Module 01

Now that you've worked through the course, rewrite it. Is the timeline realistic given your community's diagnostic? Is the position you've chosen consistent with the target? Is there a credibility-building artifact you should produce in the first six months? Specific is better than ambitious.
