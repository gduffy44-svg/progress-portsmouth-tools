Housing Action Plan — Module 12 · Triage Framework

The Capital Flow Test

Every HAP item asks Portsmouth to do something. This module asks a harder question: will it actually move capital? The city can change the rules — but only market actors, nonprofits, and individual property owners decide whether to invest. Understanding whose calculus each item changes — and how — is the most useful acid test for triage.

Capital follows return, not permission. Zoning reform is necessary but not sufficient. A city can remove every regulatory barrier and still see no investment if the underlying economics don't work. The question is never just "is this allowed?" — it's "does someone with capital find this worth doing here, at this scale, at this price point, right now?" Different actor types answer that question differently, and each responds to a different set of city levers.

The three actor tracks
Who deploys capital — and what moves them

Portsmouth's housing production challenge runs across three distinct actor types. Each has a different capital source, a different decision threshold, and a different set of conditions that tip the investment decision. HAP items that don't change the calculus for at least one of these actors are process improvements, not production drivers.

🏠
Track 1
Homeowner / Small Landlord
ADU construction, single-family to multifamily conversion, NOAH rehab and unit addition. Capital source is personal equity — HELOC, cash-out refinance, personal savings. Decisions are made slowly, risk-aversely, and are highly sensitive to permitting uncertainty.
Primary barrier
Permitting uncertainty + construction cost relative to personal risk tolerance
What moves the decision
By-right permission, pre-approved plans, fee waivers, tax abatement, accessible financing programs
What doesn't move it
LIHTC programs, nonprofit partnerships, large-scale rezoning of other areas
🔧
Track 2
Small / Private Developer
NOAH acquisition and rehab, small multifamily new construction, missing middle infill. Capital source is a mix of personal equity, commercial debt, and sometimes seller financing. Pro forma viability is the primary test — projects must pencil before commitment.
Primary barrier
Land cost + construction cost relative to achievable workforce rents = negative cash flow
What moves the decision
Seller financing, improvement grants, tax abatement (79-E), project-based vouchers, parking relief, expedited permitting
What doesn't move it
LIHTC complexity, mandatory affordability requirements without compensating incentives
🏛️
Track 3
Nonprofit / Mission Developer
LIHTC-financed permanently affordable housing, 30–60% AMI targeting, complex multi-source capital stacks. POAH, PHA, and similar organizations. Capital source is a combination of tax credit equity, soft debt, grants, and city contributions. Decision to enter a market is strategic, not opportunistic.
Primary barrier
State AHF underfunding — the operating gap cannot be closed without deep subsidy regardless of local conditions
What moves the decision
Free land (city-owned or GNOD transfer), zoning certainty, local soft money, committed city partnership, expedited review for affordable projects
What doesn't move it
Market-rate density bonuses, design standards, governance reforms

What the city controls
Direct leverage vs. enabling conditions

Not all levers are equal. Some items give the city direct control over a capital barrier. Others create enabling conditions that require an actor to respond. And some barriers are genuinely outside the city's reach — they require state action, federal funding, or macro market shifts that no local policy can substitute for.

Direct leverage — city action alone
Land disposition at below-market cost
Zoning — by-right permission for target types
Parking minimums removed by ordinance
Fee waivers formalized in ordinance
79-E program adopted by Council vote
Expedited review track established
Pre-approved ADU plan sets published
Conditional — requires actor response
Density bonuses (developer must opt in)
GNOD land transfer (developer must apply)
Nonprofit partnerships (POAH/PHA must agree)
Project-based vouchers (PHA must designate)
CDFA tax credits (businesses must contribute)
Seller financing (seller must agree)
Outside city control — state or federal action required
State Affordable Housing Fund restoration (NH Legislature)
LIHTC allocation (NH Housing — competitive statewide)
Federal Home Loan Bank AHP grants (competitive)
Section 8 / HUD voucher pool expansion (Congress)
Construction cost reduction (labor market, materials market)
Interest rate environment (Federal Reserve)
