A framework for evaluating housing policy

Three pathways. One housing puzzle.

Portsmouth's housing problem won't be solved by one approach. Each item in the city's Housing Action Plan should be evaluated against three distinct production pathways — different actors, different capital, different rules, different outputs. Items that strengthen one pathway may weaken another; the most valuable items strengthen several at once.

Where pathways overlap
01 ↔ 02
Owner-occupant + covenant
Deed-restricted ADUs and small conversions. Hard to scale; most owners decline covenants.
01 ↔ 03
Small builder + HOZ pathway
Small builders take RSA 79-E HOZ tax relief in exchange for affordability covenants. The most actionable overlap if HOZ is adopted.
02 ↔ 03
Missing middle, market-rate
Duplexes, triplexes, cottage courts. The natural overlap where small builders deliver gentle density. Politically the most contested.
01 + 02 + 03
The integration target
Local builders producing small-scale projects with affordability covenants. Stack every available tool. Currently zero such projects in Portsmouth.
Field context

How this framework relates to the broader field

The pathway framework underlying Portsmouth's Housing Action Plan integrates work that lives, elsewhere, in separate silos. The intuition that housing production happens through distinct actor types, capital sources, and rule systems isn't new. What is unusual is organizing a municipal triage process around it.

Functional taxonomy
Local Housing Solutions / NYU Furman Center
The dominant municipal framework, used by San Antonio, Philadelphia, the Bay Area's MTC, and dozens of others. Organizes policy into four categories: create dedicated affordable housing, align supply with the market, help households afford private-market homes, and protect against displacement. Cuts policy by what it does, not by who executes the production.
Small-builder thesis
Strong Towns / Incremental Development Alliance
The leading articulation of locally-rooted small-scale development as a distinct production engine. South Bend, IN is the showcase city — IDA workshops since 2017, preapproved plan catalog, a small-builder cohort that collectively rivals the largest single developer in the city. Vermont's Homes for All initiative is the strongest state-level analog, explicitly funding training and mentorship for a new generation of small-scale developers. Asks who can build at the 4–12 unit scale.
Homeowner-led density
State ADU policy stacks
Massachusetts (Affordable Homes Act, ADU Design Challenge, MassHousing financing), New York's Plus One ADU program, California's pre-approved plan mandates, and Vermont's VHIP-ADU program (up to $50,000 grants per ADU on owner-occupied property). Treats ADU and gentle-density work as a named production lane with dedicated capital and zoning infrastructure.
Mission-driven workforce
State HFA + nonprofit developer pipelines
MassHousing's Momentum Fund, Atlanta's Housing Production Fund + Urban Development Authority partnership, NYC's Neighborhood Builders Fast Track, and the Furman Center's public-developer typology. The most established lane nationally, with formal frameworks for capital stacking and city-land contribution.
Peer communities at this scale

Small cities operating across multiple lanes

Filtering peer communities to populations under 50,000 narrows the field considerably. Three small cities surface as the closest matches — operating across multiple producer types in practice, even when their plans don't formally name them as pathways.

Port Angeles, WA
~20,000 · OLYMPIC PENINSULA
Coastal city with similar size and tourism-economy pressure. 2019 Housing Action Plan organized around 13 numbered tasks. Permit-Ready Plans Program (2023) for ADUs, duplexes, and small-lot homes; Multifamily Builders Training in partnership with the local builders association and community college; Housing Pipeline Demonstration Project on city-owned land; partnerships with Habitat for Humanity and Peninsula Housing Authority. Three-lane logic without explicit pathway framing.
Brattleboro, VT
~12,000 · SOUTHERN VERMONT
Regional center, 2021 Housing Action Plan (Camoin Associates). Operating ecosystem includes Brattleboro Housing Partnerships and Windham & Windsor Housing Trust on the affordable side; M&S Development and Brunelle & Son on the local-builder side; Brattleboro Area Affordable Housing's "Apartments In Homes" homeowner ADU grant program. The cross-sector Housing Coalition of Southeastern Vermont convenes producers regularly.
Claremore, OK
~22,000 · TULSA METRO
County seat outside Tulsa, identical population to Portsmouth, very different market. 2040 Comprehensive Plan adopted late 2021 with 1,000+ residents engaged. Pattern zoning pioneer — pre-approved plan catalog, "turning dirt in 20 days." Single-pathway focus rather than multi-pathway, but featured in Strong Towns / APA's April 2026 "Competitive Advantage of Small Town USA" piece as a model for cities of this size.
What's distinctive about Portsmouth's approach

Integration, application, and civic-led governance

The framework draws from each of the four streams above. What's unusual — based on a scan of peer communities — is the integration into a single working framework, the application as a triage instrument scoring 148 individual action items, and the civic-led governance through which the framework was developed.

  • Named actor-and-capital cuts as the structural axis. Most municipal plans treat actors as implicit. The pathway framework requires a named actor type, capital source, and rule system for each lane.
  • Honest state asymmetry. Active, partially active, and aspirational are explicit pathway states. Few peer frameworks acknowledge that a named pathway doesn't yet exist as an ecosystem.
  • Used as a triage instrument, not just an organizing taxonomy. Each Housing Action Plan item is scored against all three pathways simultaneously, surfacing items that strengthen multiple pathways and items that quietly weaken one. The Furman / LHS framework is designed for organizing; it isn't a scoring tool.
  • Bridge zones named explicitly. Owner-occupant + covenant. Small builder + HOZ pathway. Missing middle, market-rate. The integration target. Each is tied to specific tools.
  • Civic-developed, city-adopted. Most national frameworks are consultant-led (Opticos, CommunityScale) or state-led (MA, NY, CA). The framework here was developed by a volunteer civic organization, the underlying plan was adopted unanimously by the City Council, and a citizen committee is now applying it. That governance pattern is itself unusual.

None of this means Portsmouth invented the underlying ideas. The contribution is synthesis and operational use, with credit owed upstream to the bodies of work named above.
