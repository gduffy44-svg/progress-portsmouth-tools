## Discussion summary

### 1. Opening framing (Tabor)

Co-chair Tabor opened by recapping the May 21 straw poll and noting that Planning staff had since prepared a ranked synthesis — the Housing Committee Ranking List (working draft, May 2026) — combining the straw poll with short-term/long-term staff comments. The Ranking List was the working document for this meeting. Tabor framed the path forward as walking through each item to capture implementation strategy and timeframe input, so staff could prepare a draft Housing Action Plan for the July 9 meeting to forward to Council.

### 2. Public comment (Gerry Duffy, Progress Portsmouth)

Tabor invited public comment from Gerry Duffy. Duffy made the following points:

- The narrative forming in the committee's work is that RSA 79-E could be a strong driver for multiple housing types — market rate, conversions, and large workforce projects — because every housing project must make financial sense or it doesn't happen.
- Two young developers based in Portsmouth have recently contacted Progress Portsmouth expressing interest in small multifamily projects, citing a civic obligation as members of the cohort most affected by the housing shortage.
- A citywide Housing Opportunity Zone has precedent (Conway). The committee would still need to pair the tax tool with by-right zoning changes for it to produce housing.
- A single overall citywide unit goal is weaker than granular ones. Examples: doubling annual ADU production from the current natural flow of roughly a dozen per year; a target of about five multifamily conversions per year; initiating two or three large-scale projects in the 18 months before the current Council term ends.
- City-owned sites already identified by the prior Housing Committee, plus the Kane land swap and potential future sites (e.g., if Walmart consolidates), are the realistic universe for true affordability at scale.
- "The city can't make affordable housing happen, but it can have a zoning and incentive package that makes this stuff more likely to happen."

### 3. RSA 79-E package discussion

The committee discussed at length what an RSA 79-E package should include and where it should apply. The recently-passed expansion (HB 1103, awaiting governor's signature) was discussed as background — both the new local-discretion language on affordability and the longer relief periods for workforce housing came up.

**Sub-types considered:**

- **Housing Opportunity Zones (HOZ).** Staff (Britz) initially suggested defining the zone as Route 1 Bypass from Traffic Circle to Memorial to Middle Bridge, expandable to include the full Gateway Zone — the part of the city "primed for redevelopment." Multiple members pushed back: why not citywide, as Conway has done (whole town except downtown and certain flood-prone areas)? The committee converged on **citywide** application, with the understanding that Council approval is required on a per-project basis, so a citywide HOZ is not a blank check.
- **Office conversion.** Confirmed for inclusion. Discussion noted office conversions are expensive (bathroom layouts, windows in bedrooms, etc.), but enabling them creates space for creative reuse. Britz noted that HB 1103, if signed, would expand "Office Conversion Zones" to broader commercial — the committee took that into account and tentatively framed inclusion as the broader category.
- **Rehabilitation of dilapidated buildings.** Confirmed for inclusion. Anchor examples discussed: the Dover old courthouse project, brewery buildings converted to apartments. A specific Portsmouth-relevant case raised was the Portsmouth district court at Parrott Avenue (lightly used, could be a candidate if its function consolidates).
- **Coastal resiliency.** **Dropped from the package on optics grounds.** Members raised the concern of "a $4 million house on the water getting a tax break." While potentially useful, the perceived inequity outweighed the housing yield — and members felt energy was better directed at the other three sub-types.

**Open design questions raised but not resolved:**

1. **AMI standard.** RSA sets 80% AMI as the ceiling for HOZ-eligible affordable units, with one-third of units restricted. Portsmouth has historically used 100% or 110% AMI (West End Yards, Sage condominiums). Stebbins-Thomas surfaced this — should Portsmouth align with the RSA 80% standard, or use its discretion (recently broadened by HB 1103) to set a different threshold? Members noted that 80% is substantially harder to underwrite than 100-110%; developers would have to adjust. No decision; flagged for the July package.
2. **Affordable share percentage.** The RSA one-third requirement was understood, but the committee can adjust under HB 1103.
3. **"But for" test interpretation.** The RSA requires Council to find that "but for" the tax relief, the project would not happen. This will be a case-by-case Council determination, but the ordinance language can shape how that finding is made (Dover's approach: up-front check, then "let it ride").
4. **Underlying zoning constraints.** HOZ does not supersede underlying zoning. A non-conforming building in an SR district still requires a variance — even to renovate the interior. This means the tax tool only helps where the zoning already permits the use. Stebbins-Thomas drew this out explicitly: "they're parallel but they're two different columns. The opportunity zones is more of a massing, and then the lot size and dimensional requirements in SRA, SRB is a specific answer."
5. **ADU experience as cautionary precedent.** A committee member noted the ADU reforms tripled applications but produced relatively few completed units — financing for individual homeowners is different from developer financing, and enabling a use is not the same as making projects pencil. Members took this as a reason to pair the tax tool with by-right zoning changes, not as a reason to avoid the tax tool.

**Motion.** A member moved to request that City staff develop a draft RSA 79-E package consisting of Housing Opportunity Zones citywide, office conversion citywide, and rehabilitation citywide. A second was offered and the motion was carried by consensus among the six members present. Tabor and others noted the count was below quorum (6 of 13, threshold 7), so this should be treated as a working direction to staff, with formal re-ratification expected at the July 9 meeting.

### 4. Implementation walk-through — items by timeframe

Britz walked the committee through the remaining items from the Ranking List, capturing implementation timing.

**Confirmed as post-Master-Plan work** (Master Plan expected late fall 2026; comprehensive zoning consultant to be hired afterward, with HAP-defined scope):

- **Z-21 — Comprehensive zoning ordinance update.** Long-term anchor. Stebbins-Thomas raised whether the consultant should be sourced from outside Rockingham Planning Commission to avoid local bias (RPC's "model ordinances" tend toward cookie-cutter language). Britz confirmed the city typically hires consultants from outside the immediate region; YouTube/Utile for the Master Plan was the precedent.
- **Z-5 — Expand multifamily zoning.** Part of the post-Master-Plan rezoning.
- **Z-8 — Lot size and dimensional requirements.** Part of the post-Master-Plan rezoning. Members discussed the five-acre minimum on Little Harbor Road as an example of dimensional rules ripe for revisiting.
- **Z-3 — Residential conversion.** Previously referred to the Planning Board, which deferred to the Master Plan.

**Confirmed for short-term action:**

- **Z-1 — Rezone outdated O/R and General Business districts.** Continue ongoing work; short-term review independent of Master Plan completion.
- **Z-10 — In-lieu fee option.** Already in zoning for the Gateway; expand to other districts. Timeframe: 1–3 months ("text amendment").
- **Z-9 — Cottage / cluster housing.** Already exists in Gateway; review requirements for possible expansion. May be folded into the comprehensive update for the larger revision.

### 5. Live/work artist space funding move (Z-14)

Tabor reported that in a recent late Council session (running to roughly 12:30 a.m. the prior Monday), Council moved the funding for the live/work artist space study **out of the CIP and into the Housing Trust Fund**. The study consultant is already lined up. Trustees still need to confirm the use fits the trust's purposes; if so, funds become available July 1. The arts and cultural plan called for a specific study on zoning and incentive pathways for artist live/work space; this funding move keeps the study moving without burdening the CIP.

### 6. Items raised but not advanced

Several items came up during the walk-through but were not advanced into the July package:

- **Workforce housing ordinance strengthening.** Britz: "I'm not entirely sure we could strengthen it" beyond what already exists; could be folded into the comprehensive zoning ordinance update audit if there are legal gaps.
- **Affordable housing overlays.** Discussed as duplicative — "trying to simplify our zoning, more overlays just forward" (Britz). Other in-progress work (dimensional rules, parking flexibility for 3+ unit multifamily) addresses similar goals.
- **Affordable housing fee waivers.** Samonas surfaced a consistency question between the existing 25% AMI threshold for fee waiver and the 33% HOZ threshold; deferred for follow-up.
- **Permitting expedite.** Tabor: "I've not ever come up with a good solution on how to expedite." Held up by statutory notice periods (three weeks for notices) and tax certification timing. Members noted Tracy Kozak (former committee member) had identified speed as the most important factor; no concrete solution proposed at this meeting.
- **Senior property tax deferral expansion.** Already exists in tiers by age (65+, with larger reduction at 80+), with income and asset caps. Reviewed annually each spring. Treated as already addressed — no expansion proposed.
- **Room rental / home sharing facilitation.** Already permitted; not regulated; removed from list (consistent with the Z-18 closure already reflected in the Explorer).
- **Seasonal worker housing.** Covered by the co-living ordinance; removed from list (consistent with the Z-19 closure already reflected in the Explorer).

### 7. Staff directive for July

Britz confirmed that Planning staff will develop a draft 79-E package and a HAP framework for the July 9 meeting, to be forwarded to Council. Tabor closed the implementation walk-through by asking members to send any additional implementation/timeframe inputs to staff before the next meeting.
