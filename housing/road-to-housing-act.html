21st Century ROAD to Housing Act

A guide to the major housing-relevant provisions, rated from the perspective of an affordable housing advocate in Portsmouth, NH.

This guide covers provisions most material to Portsmouth’s housing context. Administrative, severability, and oversight sections are included in condensed form. All 11 titles are represented.

⚠
Key context for the entire bill
Most of this bill authorizes programs that only exist if Congress later funds them. Title XI, Sec. 1102 states explicitly that no additional funds are authorized. Provisions listed as pilots, grants, or new programs require a separate appropriations vote to become real. Throughout this guide, “only if funded” is not a caveat — it is the operative condition for a significant share of the bill’s supply-side provisions.

Title 1
Improving Financial Literacy
MixedSec. 101 — Housing Counseling Reform101
▼

What it does

Allows HUD to review housing counselor performance, require retraining, and decertify counselors persistently out of compliance.

What it does not do

No new funding for counseling agencies. No expansion of who can access services. Oversight without investment in capacity.

Advocate readQuality control is reasonable, but counseling agencies are already understaffed. More oversight without more resources may strain thin capacity. Net effect is marginal.
Title 2
Building More in America
HelpfulSec. 201 — RAD Cap LiftedPortsmouth201
▼

What it does

Removes the cap on the Rental Assistance Demonstration program, allowing PHAs to convert public housing to project-based rental assistance and unlock private rehabilitation financing. Codifies tenant protections including relocation rights and right to return.

Portsmouth relevance

Portsmouth Housing Authority manages 650+ public housing units. An uncapped RAD program gives PHA more flexibility to pursue private rehabilitation financing — though no specific RAD conversion is confirmed in recent public documents.

Advocate readOne of the stronger provisions in the bill. RAD has a track record as a preservation mechanism. Codifying tenant protections alongside the cap lift addresses the historic concern that conversions could displace residents.
MixedSec. 202 — HUD Grants Weighted Toward Opportunity Zones202
▼

What it does

Allows HUD to give priority in competitive grant competitions to applicants in or serving federal Opportunity Zones.

The problem

The OZ framework created in 2017 has a mixed affordability record — many investments went to market-rate development. Tying HUD housing grants to an OZ preference inherits that ambiguity.

Advocate readThe intent — directing resources toward disinvested areas — is reasonable. Impact depends on how HUD implements the preference and whether any affordability conditions attach.
HelpfulSec. 203 — Whole-Home Repairs ActPortsmouth203
▼

What it does

Creates a pilot offering grants and forgivable loans for deferred maintenance and health hazards in aging homes — for both homeowners and landlords — stabilizing housing stock across tenure types.

Portsmouth relevance

Portsmouth's housing stock is old: roughly 73% of owner-occupied units were built before 1980, 61% before 1960. Preservation of aging rental properties is as important locally as owner-occupied homes — both tenure types are relevant here.

Advocate readPreservation is often cheaper than new construction. This provision targets affordability-through-stability across both tenure types. Strong, if funded.
HelpfulSec. 204 — Community Investment Cap Raised for Banks204
▼

What it does

Raises the Public Welfare Investment cap for nationally chartered banks from 15% to 20% of capital, expanding private investment capacity for LIHTC, CDFIs, and other affordable housing vehicles.

What it means

More private capital can flow into affordable housing financing without requiring federal appropriations. Benefits are diffuse but directionally correct.

Advocate readA quiet but meaningful provision. Expands the private capital pool for affordable projects at no budget cost.
MixedSec. 205 and 206 — CDBG: Production Pilot and New Construction EligibilityPortsmouth205-206
▼

What it does

Sec. 206 adds new construction as an eligible CDBG use — previously not permitted. Sec. 205 is more complex: it ties some CDBG funding to housing production outcomes, including bonuses for faster building and modest reductions for grantees that lag. This is a performance regime, not just expanded flexibility.

Portsmouth relevance

Portsmouth is a CDBG entitlement city. Using CDBG for new affordable construction is a genuine improvement. The Sec. 205 performance structure adds scrutiny — cities facing local political barriers to production could be exposed to funding adjustments depending on how HUD defines production benchmarks in rulemaking.

Advocate readOpening CDBG to new construction is overdue and clearly positive. The Sec. 205 performance regime is more politically charged than the headline suggests — not just more flexibility but accountability with mild consequences. For Portsmouth, actively working on housing production, that trade-off is probably manageable.
MixedSec. 207 and 208 — Environmental Review Streamlining207-208
▼

What it does

Sec. 207 empowers state and local governments to streamline federal environmental reviews. Sec. 208 right-sizes NEPA review specifically for small and infill housing projects.

The tension

Environmental review protects communities — especially lower-income ones — from harmful development. Sec. 208’s infill focus is more defensible. Sec. 207’s broader delegation warrants close attention in rulemaking.

Advocate readRight-sizing NEPA for small infill projects makes sense and could help unlock missing-middle housing. Broader streamlining language often travels further in implementation than authors intend.
HelpfulSec. 209, 210, 211 — Planning Grants, Innovation Fund, Pattern Books209-211
▼

What it does

Competitive grants for regional housing planning (209); flexible funding for communities actively building supply (210); pre-approved design pattern books to speed permitting (211).

What to watch

All three are unfunded pilot authorizations. Title XI includes a “No Additional Funds Authorized” clause — these programs exist only if separately appropriated.

Advocate readThe tools are right. Pattern books could help Portsmouth move faster on ADU and missing-middle housing. But these programs require appropriations to exist, which is far from guaranteed.
HelpfulSec. 212 — RESIDE Act: Vacant Buildings to HousingPortsmouth212
▼

What it does

Creates a pilot within the HOME program to convert vacant and abandoned buildings into attainable housing, providing federal subsidy for adaptive reuse projects that often require public support to pencil.

Portsmouth relevance

Adaptive reuse proposals have surfaced in Portsmouth’s urban core — including discussions around 134 Pleasant Street and the former J.J. Newberry building. This provision could be useful where such opportunities emerge, though a systematic local vacancy case has not been established.

Advocate readAdaptive reuse typically requires public subsidy to work. This creates a vehicle for that. Potentially useful for Portsmouth on a project-by-project basis as opportunities surface downtown.
HelpfulSec. 213 — FHA Multifamily Loan Limit Increase213
▼

What it does

Requires FHA to increase multifamily loan limits to better match actual housing market costs, restoring FHA-backed multifamily financing viability in high-cost markets.

Why it matters

When loan limits lag construction costs, FHA financing is effectively unavailable in expensive markets. This is a structural fix for the Seacoast region.

Advocate readQuiet but important. Updating limits to match Seacoast construction costs makes a key affordable apartment financing tool actually usable locally.
Title 3
Manufactured Housing for America
MixedSec. 301 — Manufactured Housing Definition Expanded; Energy Standards Paused301
▼

What it does

Broadens the legal definition of manufactured housing to include units not built on a permanent chassis, expanding factory-built options. Also blocks energy efficiency standards from taking effect until formally adopted by HUD.

The problem

Pausing energy standards could mean lower-income households — who disproportionately live in manufactured homes — face higher utility costs for years. Energy efficiency is an affordability issue. Two distinct policy choices are bundled here.

Advocate readThe definition expansion is a supply win. The energy standard pause is a real cost to the residents those homes serve. Both deserve to be named clearly.
MixedSec. 302 and 303 — Modular and Manufactured Housing Financing Studies302-303
▼

What it does

Directs FHA to assess lending barriers for modular housing and expand financing access for manufactured homes. Includes a cost-effectiveness study of factory-built housing finance.

The limitation

These are study and assessment directives. Financing barriers for manufactured and modular housing are already well-documented — the gap is action, not additional research.

Advocate readFactory-built housing is a legitimate affordability pathway. The financing barriers are well-documented; directing HUD to study them is a weaker response than the scale of the problem warrants. Limited direct Portsmouth relevance, though factory-built housing could ease regional workforce housing production in outlying Seacoast communities.
HelpfulSec. 304 — PRICE Act: Protecting Manufactured Housing Communities304
▼

What it does

Authorizes HUD’s PRICE Program, providing grants to preserve and stabilize manufactured housing communities and protect residents from displacement when parks are sold or closed.

Why it matters

Manufactured housing communities are a major source of naturally occurring affordable housing. When parks close, residents lose both their home and often their primary asset.

Advocate readAnti-displacement protection for a housing population consistently underrepresented in federal policy. Unambiguously positive, even with limited direct Portsmouth relevance.

▼

What it does

Allows licensed (not just credentialed) appraisers to conduct FHA appraisals, expanding the workforce and potentially reducing delays.

What to watch

Appraisal bias is a documented problem. Expanding the workforce without improving training standards could amplify existing inequities. This addresses delays, not accuracy and bias.

HelpfulSec. 404 — Family Self-Sufficiency Homeownership Pilot404
▼

What it does

Expands HUD’s Family Self-Sufficiency program to help more families build savings and work toward homeownership as an economic mobility pathway.

Why it matters

FSS has a solid evidence base for helping voucher-holders build assets. Homeownership is one of the few legal wealth-building mechanisms accessible to lower-income households, though Portsmouth’s price levels put it out of reach for most FSS participants.

Advocate readEquity-positive. Addresses part of the historical wealth gap created by discriminatory housing policy. The policy direction is sound even where the local pathway is narrow.
HelpfulSec. 405 — Voucher Inspection ReformPortsmouth405
▼

What it does

Units inspected by another federal program within the past year automatically satisfy voucher inspection requirements. Landlords can also request pre-inspections before a tenant is placed.

Portsmouth relevance

Landlord reluctance to participate in the voucher program is a documented barrier in high-demand markets like Portsmouth. Reducing inspection friction removes a real pain point — though it does not address the deeper problem of payment standards lagging market rents.

Advocate readPractical and meaningful. Inspection delays discourage landlord participation, limiting options for the lowest-income households. A genuine improvement within the current system.

What it does

Makes CDBG-Disaster Recovery permanent law rather than requiring ad hoc Congressional authorization after each disaster. Creates a dedicated HUD office to administer it.

Why it matters

Permanent authorization means faster, more predictable disaster recovery response — removing the political bottleneck that has previously delayed housing recovery for years following major disasters.

Advocate readStrong structural fix. Permanent authorization removes a known failure mode from the federal disaster housing response system.

Reforms and reauthorizes the HOME Investment Partnerships Program with updates aimed at improving administration and facilitating more affordable housing construction.

Portsmouth relevance

HOME dollars flow through NH Housing to municipalities and nonprofits, including Portsmouth-area projects. More and better-administered HOME is a direct local benefit.

HelpfulSec. 503 — Rural Housing Service Reform503
▼

What it does

Decouples rental assistance from expiring USDA mortgages, preserving affordable housing in rural areas. Protects an estimated 400,000 rural families from displacement when project mortgages mature.

Portsmouth relevance

Limited direct impact locally, but relevant to the broader NH Seacoast region. Workforce housing shortages in outlying towns affect the regional labor supply Portsmouth’s economy depends on.

Advocate readImportant anti-displacement provision at scale for markets that receive even less policy attention than Portsmouth. Indirect relevance via regional workforce housing dynamics: housing stability in outlying NH communities affects who can afford to work in Portsmouth.
MixedSec. 504 — New Moving to Work Cohort504
▼

What it does

Authorizes a new Moving to Work cohort allowing participating housing authorities to waive certain HUD requirements in exchange for testing new program approaches.

The tension

MTW has generated genuine innovation in some PHAs and has been used to implement self-sufficiency requirements critics argue harm vulnerable residents. Outcomes depend entirely on which authorities participate and what waivers they pursue.

Advocate readA neutral tool that produces good or bad outcomes depending on implementation. Monitor what specific waivers participating PHAs request.
HelpfulSec. 505 — Homelessness Funding Flexibility505
▼

What it does

Allows states and localities receiving Emergency Solutions Grant funding to request a waiver of the statutory 60% spending cap on emergency shelter and street outreach — giving communities more flexibility to respond to homelessness as local conditions require.

Why it matters

The 60% cap was designed to push communities toward prevention over emergency shelter. A waiver pathway creates flexibility without eliminating the underlying policy preference.

Advocate readSensible flexibility provision. Gives localities room to respond to actual conditions rather than statutory proportionality requirements that may not fit the local pattern of need.
ConcernSec. 803 — Study on Work Requirements in Public Housing803
▼

What it does

Directs HUD to study the implementation of work requirements by public housing agencies, assessing effects on homelessness, poverty, job attainment, and administrative capacity. It orders a study — not work requirements themselves.

Why advocates are wary

The broader evidence on work requirements in housing and safety-net programs — documented repeatedly by CBPP — shows they tend to increase housing instability without improving long-term employment outcomes. In federal housing policy, a mandated study is typically the precursor to policy expansion.

Advocate readThis provision does not impose work requirements. A study is not a mandate. The concern is that mandated research in this area has historically preceded policy expansion — and the weight of existing evidence on work requirements in housing programs, documented by CBPP and others, is already substantial. Advocates should treat this as an early indicator of where the policy conversation is heading.

Veterans and Housing
HelpfulSec. 601, 602, 603 — Veterans Housing Access and HUD-VASH Reform601-603
▼

What it does

Adds VA loan disclosures to standard mortgage applications. Permanently excludes veterans’ disability compensation from income calculations under HUD-VASH, helping disabled homeless veterans qualify for housing assistance.

Why it matters

The HUD-VASH fix removes a regulatory barrier that was actively preventing some of the most vulnerable veterans from accessing housing. Many veterans are also unaware of their VA home loan benefit, which often offers better terms than FHA.

Advocate readThe HUD-VASH income exclusion is the most substantive provision here — permanently removing a rule that blocked disabled homeless veterans from housing access. The disclosure requirements are straightforward improvements.

HelpfulSec. 704 — Appraisal Reconsideration of ValuePortsmouth704
▼

What it does

Requires USDA, FHA, and FHFA to implement formal procedures allowing consumers to request a second appraisal or reconsideration of value when they believe their home has been undervalued.

Why it matters

Appraisal bias is a documented mechanism through which housing wealth disparities are perpetuated — disproportionately affecting communities of color. A formal reconsideration process creates a procedural check on undervaluation without requiring a full regulatory overhaul.

Advocate readA meaningful procedural safeguard that creates a formal check on appraisal disputes, including cases of potential bias. It does not structurally reform how appraisals are conducted, but it gives consumers a documented path to challenge valuations they believe are inaccurate.

Title 9
Homeownership for Main Street America
MixedSec. 901 — Institutional Investor Restrictions on Single-Family HomesPortsmouth901
▼

What it does

Prohibits large institutional investors from purchasing certain single-family homes. The Senate-passed language goes beyond the White House’s original proposal by attaching a disposition requirement to newly built homes — requiring investors to sell within roughly seven years — not just restricting purchases of existing stock.

The rental production concern

Multifamily rental communities are typically financed on long hold structures, with investors underwriting returns over 15–20 years. A forced seven-year sale window raises required returns and can make new deals financially unviable. Bipartisan House members including Financial Services Chair French Hill and Ranking Member Maxine Waters have raised concerns. Industry sources report underwriting assumptions in to-be-built communities are being revisited, though systematic evidence of a capital freeze remains more anecdotal than documented.

Portsmouth relevance

Institutional buying competes with first-time buyers in the Seacoast market — restricting that is positive. But Portsmouth also needs new family-sized rental production. If the disposition requirement deters new rental construction capital, the city loses on both ends. The existing-vs.-new distinction is the crux of what House negotiations need to resolve.

What a fix looks like

The most viable path in Hill discussions: apply the investor restriction to existing single-family home purchases only, with a carveout for capital financing new rental construction. The seven-year disposition requirement is the specific provision that proposed fixes target.

Advocate read — updated April 2, 2026Downgraded from Helpful (v1.0) based on Senate-passed language. Restricting corporate purchases of existing homes is sound policy with clear homeownership benefits. Attaching a forced-sale timeline to new rental construction capital is a different intervention. The concern about chilling new rental supply is credible enough to warrant a targeted fix — and the fix is achievable without gutting the original intent.

UnrelatedSec. 1101 and 1102 — Severability and No Additional Funds1101-1102
▼

What it does

Sec. 1101 is a standard severability clause. Sec. 1102 states that no additional funds are authorized by the bill.

Why Sec. 1102 matters

The “No Additional Funds Authorized” clause is significant context for the entire bill. Many provisions — pilot programs, planning grants, innovation funds, pattern books — are authorizations requiring separate appropriations. Authorization without appropriation is common. This is why the phrase “only if funded” recurs throughout this guide.

Advocate readSec. 1101 is boilerplate. Sec. 1102 is the single most important context for reading the bill’s actual impact potential.

What this bill does well

  • Lifts the RAD cap and codifies tenant protections (Sec. 201)
  • Opens CDBG to new affordable construction (Sec. 206)
  • Addresses adaptive reuse financing via RESIDE (Sec. 212)
  • Permanently authorizes CDBG-DR for disaster recovery (Sec. 501)
  • Restricts institutional investors from buying existing homes — if Sec. 901 is fixed in the House
  • Reforms and reauthorizes HOME program (Sec. 502)
  • Reduces voucher inspection friction (Sec. 405)
  • Establishes appraisal reconsideration rights (Sec. 704)
  • Permanently protects disabled veterans in HUD-VASH (Sec. 603)
  • ESG flexibility for homelessness response (Sec. 505)

What is missing or concerning

  • Sec. 901 Senate language risks chilling new rental construction — a House fix is needed
  • No expansion of Section 8 or Housing Choice Voucher program
  • No new LIHTC allocations or 4% LIHTC fix
  • Sec. 803 work requirements study is a political signal, not neutral research
  • Many provisions are unfunded pilot authorizations (see Sec. 1102)
  • No rent stabilization or national tenant protection floor
  • Energy standard pause in Sec. 301 harms manufactured housing residents
  • Sec. 205 performance regime could penalize cities facing local production barriers
